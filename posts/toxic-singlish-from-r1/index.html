Our Prompting Approach

Our strategy revolved around a single, carefully crafted prompt that explicitly defined six harm categories ‚Äî such as ‚Äúhate speech,‚Äù ‚Äúinsults,‚Äù or other forms of toxicity ‚Äî and provided two to three explicit examples for each. This prompt guided the model to generate realistic yet negative statements.

In essence, our prompt used:

  1. Clear instructions for handling ambiguity (e.g., how to distinguish hate speech from general insults).
  2. Text delimiters (e.g., XML tags) to separate instructions from content or examples.
  3. Examples of harmful phrases ‚Äî not all strictly Singlish ‚Äî to illustrate the style and content we sought.

Notably, we did not deploy any specific ‚Äújailbreaking‚Äù methods. Rather, we relied on standard best practices for prompt engineering ‚Äî providing well-structured, unambiguous instructions that guided the model toward producing the outputs we wanted.

For safety reasons, we are not sharing the full original prompt in this blog post.

Why this matters

These episodic findings highlight how no model or system is invulnerable, and systematic red-teaming or testing is essential to uncover hidden blind spots before they manifest in real-world deployments.

Additionally, as AI models or systems become more sophisticated, solely relying on manual red-teaming becomes impractical. Leveraging LLMs for automated red-teaming ‚Äî where models probe other models ‚Äî represents a powerful new avenue. That r1 was able to produce such toxic and realistic statements in the first place underscores both: (i) the urgency of ongoing safety work in AI, and (ii) the potential of leveraging them to enhance our safety efforts.
